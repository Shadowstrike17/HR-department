Workforce Planning

Workforce planning involves calculating the number and types of staff that will be required by a business. This includes:

  • Forecasting future staffing needs
  • Determining skills and qualifications required
  • Planning for recruitment or redundancy
  • Ensuring the right staff are available at the right time

In small businesses, this process is less complex, but for large organizations employing thousands, effective workforce planning is crucial to meet customer demands.

Recruitment and Selection

Recruitment and selection is the process of finding and hiring the best qualified candidate for a job vacancy. This typically involves:

  • Planning workforce needs
  • Creating and placing job advertisements
  • Providing application forms
  • Selecting appropriate candidates for interview
  • Conducting interviews
  • Selecting the best employees for the position

The HR department typically manages this process, though final selection may involve managers from the departments where new employees will work.

Contract of Employment

A contract of employment is a written agreement between an employer and an employee that establishes the rights and obligations of both parties. It typically includes:

  • Job title and description
  • Salary and payment terms
  • Working hours and location
  • Holiday entitlement
  • Sick leave provisions
  • Notice period
  • Disciplinary and grievance procedures

The HR department is responsible for creating these contracts, explaining their content to new employees, and ensuring both parties sign copies.

Importance of Training

Advantages of Training:

  • Improves employee skills and productivity
  • Increases employee confidence and job satisfaction
  • Helps adapt to new technologies and processes
  • Reduces employee turnover and recruitment costs
  • Ensures compliance with health and safety standards

Disadvantages of Training:

  • Can be expensive to implement
  • Requires time away from productive work
  • Trained employees may leave for better positions elsewhere
  • Training may not transfer effectively to workplace tasks

What are the Benefits and Drawbacks of Redundancy?
Click to flip
Redundancy: Benefits & Drawbacks

Potential Benefits for a Business:

  • Reduces wage costs in the long term
  • Can help a struggling business survive
  • Opportunity to restructure operations
  • May improve efficiency with fewer staff

Drawbacks:

  • High initial redundancy payment costs
  • Loss of skilled and experienced workers
  • Negative impact on staff morale
  • Potential damage to company reputation
  • Remaining staff may face increased workload
  • Possible legal challenges if procedures aren't followed correctly

Health and Safety Importance

Advantages of Good Health and Safety:

  • Reduces workplace accidents and injuries
  • Decreases employee absence due to illness
  • Avoids potential legal penalties and compensation claims
  • Improves employee morale and productivity
  • Enhances company reputation with stakeholders

Disadvantages/Challenges:

  • Implementation costs for training and equipment
  • Time required for safety procedures
  • Ongoing monitoring and updates needed
  • Can create additional administrative burden

How might poor industrial relations affect a business?

Impact of Poor Industrial Relations

Poor industrial relations between management and employees/trade unions can have several significant impacts:

  • Industrial action - strikes or work-to-rule can disrupt production and affect customer service
  • Lower productivity - demotivated workers may reduce their effort or quality of work
  • Higher staff turnover - employees may leave, increasing recruitment costs
  • Negative publicity - disputes can damage the company's reputation
  • Difficulty implementing change - resistance to new procedures or technologies
  • Legal costs - potential expenses from employment tribunals or legal disputes

A strong HR department plays a crucial role in maintaining positive industrial relations through effective communication, fair policies, and consistent handling of grievances.

Disciplinary Procedure

A typical disciplinary procedure managed by HR will follow these steps:

  1. Informal discussion - For minor issues, a manager may have an informal conversation with the employee
  2. Investigation - If the issue is serious or persists, HR will gather evidence and information
  3. Formal meeting - The employee is invited to a meeting with written notice and right to bring a representative
  4. Verbal warning - First formal stage, usually recorded but expires after a set period
  5. Written warning - More serious or repeated issues lead to formal written warnings
  6. Final written warning - For continued problems or serious misconduct
  7. Dismissal - The last resort for gross misconduct or continued failure to improve
